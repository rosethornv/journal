Yesterday, I started watching one of my Domestika courses that I mentioned on Thursday link

It was from Andrea Chapela - intro to writing sci-fi and fantasy. She started her career in writing at the age of 12 writing fan fiction of Harry Potter. Like for so many millennials, Harry Potter is also a huge influence of mine as well.

She said that because of this start in fan fic, writing for her has always been a public process and never a private one. It is meant to be shared.

For me, this triggered something. I don’t mind sharing my writing, but the boundary between what a person keeps personal versus shared publicly - it’s different for everyone. Every time I write on Medium, I worry about making it presentable and polished.

I have done a few visual art courses on Domestika and read about visual artists’ processes. I feel like visual art it is more common to be open to sharing “ugly” first attempts and the many stages of creating a finished piece. How much work it is crafting layer upon layer of artwork.

The process of creating written work is not quite as Instagramable. Writing and editing and rewriting is generally more of a hidden process. I suppose it doesn’t have to be. But it just doesn’t captivate people’s imagination the same way that watching an artist draw does.

Fan fiction forums and peer review groups and beta readers - these are all ways to get feedback. But I suppose part of the issue is that it takes much longer focus and attention for a written piece for the quality and depth to be revealed. It cannot be time-lapsed the way that a painting can.

For writing here in my personal space, versus writing on Medium, I can leave in all of my “I suppose”s and my crappy sentences poorly constructed sentences without shame. I suppose that this online notebook is like an artist’s sketchbook.

I can be honest. I can be myself. And some people won’t like me. But other people will resonate. And as a member of humanity, it is nice to find other humans who have had similar experiences. It makes us feel a sense of connection which is as important to the heart as emotional food, just as regular edible food nourishes our bodies.

I don’t particularly like the idea of putting this behind a paywall on Substack. For me, it makes this writing feel like dirty laundry being sold at auction to the highest bidder.

Last night, I wanted to delete the section that I wrote yesterday in reference to my half-sister.

When I was in therapy, we talked about the nature of secrets. What is ok to keep a secret? What is not ok? My half-sister was/is a very controlling person. I too can be controlling in my own way. This is much of what Al-Anon and Codependency literature revolves around.

I think that familial relationships are much more likely to have deep issues around control versus freedom, especially in “closed family systems” which are quite tribal - very “us versus the world.” So part of that still lingers in my mind. Even though she was very cruel to me, a part of me still feels like she is still part of me and we are against the world.

She was a very possessive person and didn’t want me having any outside friends or relationships. She viewed virtually everyone as her enemy.

That narrative doesn’t work anymore though. So I haven’t deleted it.

When I want to write about my half-sister, it is important that it is not all about her. That’s how I lived most of my first 30 years was all about her and her feelings. I cared about her deeply but I am trying to move forward with my life. Managing this transition period is a big part of my current existence. I asked ChatGPT for a no-nonsense pep talk on this topic.

One of the key lines was “she is just going to be a footnote in my life.” That may well be true when I am an old woman. However, at this point, I have spent 90% of my life with her. She can hardly be relegated to a footnote just yet.

Complex feelings about not saying sorry all the time.
